    Subsection Proof by Cases

    Idea: ``Proof by Cases" is a type of direct proof. We use proof by cases when we might not have enough hypotheses (like the whole hypothesis is ``\(n\) is an integer"), or if the conclusion suggests cases (like ``...is even" or ``...is a type 1 integer"). Proof by Cases works when you break down the problem and do ``sub proofs" that will `` cover" the whole hypothesis. E.g.., if the hypothesis is ``\(n\) is an integer" then you could do 2 sub proofs - one where \(n\) is an even integer and one where \(n\) is an odd integer. Since all integers are either even or odd, you’ve proven the result for all integers!

    Subsection Divisibility

    Definition: A nonzero integer \(a\) is said to divide an integer \(b\) if \(b = ak\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) When \(a\) does divide \(b\text{,}\) we write ``\(a\mid b\)" and when \(a\) does not divide \(b\text{,}\) we write ``\(a\nmid b\)".
    🔗

    Activity 9.

    With your team, prove one of the following propositions:
    🔗
    (a)
    For all integers \(m\) and \(n\text{,}\) if \(m\mid n\) then \(m\mid (7n^3 + 13n^2 - n)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    For all integers \(m\) and \(n\text{,}\) if \(m\mid n\text{,}\) then \(m^2\mid n^2\text{.}\)

    For all integers \(a, b\) and \(c\text{,}\) and all integers \(m\) and \(n\text{,}\) if \(a\mid b\) and \(a\mid c\) then \(a\mid bm+cn\text{.}\)

    Theorem 12.

    If \(x\) is an odd integer and \(y\) is an even integer, then \(x \cdot y\) is an even integer.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Assume that \(x\) is an odd integer and \(y\) is an even integer. We will show that \(x \cdot y\) is an even integer using a direct proof. Since \(x\) is an odd integer, by definition of ``odd" there exists some integer \(k\) such that \(x = 2k+1\text{.}\) Since \(y\) is an even integer, by definition of ``even" there exists some \(m \in \Z\) such that \(y = 2m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Using algebra, we have: \begin{align*} xy &= (2k+1)(2m) &= 4km+2m &= 2(2km+m). \end{align*}
    🔗
    Because \(k\) and \(m\) are integers, and the integers are closed under addition and multiplication, we know that \(f = 2km+m\) is also an integer. Thus the previous equation has demonstrated how to write \(xy\) in the form \(xy = 2f\text{,}\) where \(f \in \Z\text{.}\) By definition, this shows that \(xy\) is an even integer. Consequently, we have shown that if \(x\) is an odd integer and \(y\) is an even integer, then \(x \cdot y\) is an even integer.
