    Subsection Vocabulary and Notation

    |c|p{4.25in}|} Word Symbol Meaning
    Statement A statement is a sentence or mathematical expression that is true or false.
    Open Sentence An open sentence is a sentence or mathematical expression which does not have a truth value, depends on some unknown, becomes a statement when the unknown is specified.
    And \(\wedge\)
    Or \(\vee\) \(P\wedge Q\) is true when both \(P\) and \(Q\) are true.
    Not (or Negation) \(\sim\) \(P \vee Q\) is true when at least one of \(P\) or \(Q\) is true.
    Conditional Statement (``If...then") \(\implies\) \(P\implies Q\) means ``If \(P\) then \(Q\)" and is only false in situations where \(P\) is true and \(Q\) is false.
    Hypothesis In a conditional statement ``If \(P\) then \(Q\)", \(P\) is the hypothesis.
    Conclusion In a conditional statement ``If \(P\) then \(Q\)", \(Q\) is the conclusion.
    Biconditional Statement ``if and only if", or iff \(\Leftrightarrow\) A biconditional statement is of the form \(P\) if and only if \(Q\text{.}\) It means \(P\implies Q\) and \(Q\implies P\text{.}\)
    Converse The converse of \(P\implies Q\) is \(Q\implies P\text{.}\)
    Logically Equivalent \(\Leftrightarrow\) A statement is logically equivalent to another statement if they have the same truth value on all possible inputs. E.g., \(\sim (P \wedge Q) \Leftrightarrow \sim P \,\vee \sim Q\) by DeMorgan’s Laws
    For all \(\forall\) Also, ``For ever", ``For each"
    There exists \(\exists\) Also ``For some"
    Quantifier Indicates the number or quantity of our objects. \(\forall\) is the universal quantifier and \(\exists\) is the existential quantifier
    Tautology A statement that is true no matter what, e.g., \(S\vee \sim S\)
    Contradiction A statement that is never true, e.g., \(S\wedge \sim S\)
    Converse The converse of \(P\implies Q\) is \(Q\implies P\text{.}\)
    Contrapositive The contrapositive of \(P\implies Q\) is \(\sim Q \implies \sim P\text{.}\)

    Subsection Negating Conditional Statements

    The negation of the statement \(P\) is the statement ``not \(P\)" and is denoted by \(\sim P\text{.}\) The negation of \(P\text{,}\) \(\sim P\text{,}\) is true only when \(P\) is false, and \(\sim P\) is false only when \(P\) is true.
    🔗
    We want to be able to write the negations of statements written in English, so that we can get the statement of opposite truth value. This will be helpful when doing proofs by contradiction.
    🔗
